Hi Jack sensei,

I would like to say a big Thank You / Arigato about having me in your firing crew and welcoming me in your hometown. I'm very proud to be part of the process, but at the same time, I feel that I shouldn't be satisfied merely with the fact that I'm wood-firing my works. When I started last year, I asked the same question to my instructor, and he said "that's good thing, many people just satisfied firing their works in anagama". Well, I'm going back to improving my forms and basic skills. The funny thing is, I remember that you were already planning next firing. Then I realized that I have a bunch of new ideas for my next firing as well. It's like waiting for Christmas Gift every other month now.

I came back to NYC, and now I'm shifting gears to New England Wood Firing Conference. I picked a place Alison Palmer's studio for my ticket, and she taught me how to load kilns so I spent two days in Kent, CT. I remember that you mentioned Ken Matuzaki's workshop next year in her workshop, so I guess I will see you around! I will deliver your chrome magnesite kiln supports tomorrow. I'll do another shift at Trevor's big Anagama on this Friday.

Thanks, Kiichi,

Trevor wrote about the firing, and will send images when he has time.

You are a valuable asset at firings, and must have been kept busy and happy, learning so much information wherever you go.

I wonder if you are developing preferences about how you hope your pieces come out. (If not, no worries because you have the rest of your life for such things.) Each time we fire with so many different people, each of whom is using different clays, we are presented with options and choice-making based on what we see at unloading time. I think such bewildering options must be very confusing to traditional potters whose work has a certain "look" that is financially successful. Maybe it isn't a contemporary problem, but it might have been in the 1970's when American and Japanese potters were passing each other in the air; the American potters missed a tradition they never had and wanted to get exposure to it; the Japanese potters feeling stultified by tradition, and wanting to be free of it in the manner of what the Americans were up to. Maybe by now things have comparatively settled down. The 70's were a lively time, with much discussion about people trying to make "Japanese" pots, and others criticizing them for not making them "Japanese" enough, and still others being critical for even trying. One friend of mine posed for pictures of himself wearing Japanese traditional clothing surrounded by his pots.

Very lively discussions took place about "appropriation" in those days when people discussed "American wood firing," as if there were such a thing, and U. S. potters exhibiting in Japan, succeeding to get big prices for their work, only to try and fail at charging so much at home, and then, when Japanese collectors visited their studios, becoming very confused about whether to charge "Japanese" prices while the visitors were there. Lots of humor about such things but only after lot of confusion. You may be lucky for missing those times, but no matter; learning is not a steady, seamless process; it's more like hitting a brick wall, bouncing off, and catching a tailwind.

The teacher I respected most was Bill Daley, who was impish about such things. In the 1980's I told him I was so enthusiastic about firing with wood that I wanted to go to Japan and learn from masters. He wrote back, saying, "Don't go to Japan! Whatever you do don't go! You'll show up with your empty knowledge bowl and hold it out, saying, "Please put knowledge in my empty bowl," but some people might only put in the bowl what they want you to have. Stay home and learn what your firings will teach, so when you go - and you will know when it's time - you and those potters will share information and everyone will benefit."

I was stunned, but followed his advice. In 1991 I joined the IWCAT program (International Workshop in Ceramic Art at Tokoname) and lived with the Takeuchi family (Two sisters, one of whose husband had died, and Naoe "Now-way" who taught ballet.

They were so kind to me, offering me a 6-tatami room, and giving me breakfast, dinner, and a bicycle to ride 2 miles to a junior high school where about 21 of us from 7 countries worked every day on wheels that had been brought in, along with so much clay we couldn't possibly use it all. Potters gave us demonstrations, we visited museums and studios, fired in many different kilns, and everybody gained weight from all the meals we ate so happily.

I will never forget visiting the excavation site of 11 anagama kilns from 1,000 years ago being revealed on a hillside outside Tokoname. It had such an emotional impact I didn't know if my heart could expand enough to take it all in. Such moments in my education have profoundly impacted me to such an extent that I truly believe this life is just about learning, and definitely not about "having a career" and "Being Successful." Sometime when you come back in cold weather we can visit the remains of an 1867 salt kiln in the woods by the railroad not far from here. Two friends and I located it about 100 years after the potter had been working. (Of course in this country that is like "ancient history.") Scratching around for shards of his pots and looking at the fireboxes glazed in place is always thrilling, like visiting a ghost-place.

It has always been sad to me that the last potters working in Huntingdon stopped about 50 years before I came to town, so I never met anyone who knew them personally. What is more, every one of them died thinking pottery making as they knew it had been replaced by industrial processes emphasizing speed, economics, mass-production, heartlessness, and then came the glass industry. Who wouldn't want a glass jug instead of an opaque clay one with a drowned mouse inside trying to get molasses?

End of story for today.

As always, best wishes, and good health,

Jack

Hi Jack sensei,

I appreciate your generous encouragement, and great story in the email. I'm glad to read your own history of ceramic art and learning experience, especially the interactions between different countries. The reason that I was so interested in this field was because when I noticed inter-cultural story like yours or Mingei.

In fall 2021, I was casually enjoying learning pottery since my wife who is art major introduced me to a class at LIU Brooklyn campus. I was just continuing in a private studio during the following winter break around the beginning of 2022. One day, my instructor suddenly uttered "This is Minnesota Style!" during her demo. Because she just made the bowl's rim thicker and curved a few thin lines so that it is easy to grab.

Students understood the concept of balance between functional ware and aesthetic beauty, but why Minesota? Nobody else picked it up in the session but I researched it at home, then I found that it was about "Minnesota" by Warren MacKenzie. Somewhat her word remained in my head for while, then I read a story about Bernerd Leach. I explored more about Mingei movement by Hamada / Yanagi / Kawai. I read more about Rosanjin who argue about Mingei but they all study from Korea and China. Rosanjin even went to Korea to visit ancient kiln sites, like you did in Tokoname.

(I thought this is easy way to answer your question about my preference development but let me continue. You can scroll to the bottom haha )

When I search keyword "Anagama" surprisingly more information coming up from this country in the American Style Pottery domain. I found a retired instructor at our University who knows wood-fire people, then I started last June at Trevor's place. People around me had impression of what I do is "Kiichi started traditional pottery in ancient Japanese wood firing method because that's his blood" - it could be partially true since I was born in Japan, but I disagree when people see me in that way. I spent half of my life in this country. But if you remember, I grew up in Tokyo and I came to study computer science in the U.S. I don't carry that much traditions in my background.

I'm taking Anagama Firing as contemporary art process, and it bloomed in American Potters because of efforts from people like you. We chose Anagama as a tool to achieve something because none of the other methods can do this. I often ask questions to myself, why I'm doing this? I am fascinated by this medium so that I can develop notions in ceramic art. Clay is a very unique medium which remains another 1000 years after firing in anagama. You can view the art, you can touch it, and even you can lick it. There are no such art objects you can lick. Obviously, you don't want to lick Mona Lisa but we appreciate drinking mugi-cha with Hamada's yunomi.

However, the difficult / fun / challenging part of this art world is, we have to draw lines somewhere as you pointed out impact of industrialization. Anagama is actually lost technology. Japanese didn't do this for 400 years since Nobori-Gama technology arrived from Korea in late 1500-ish because Anagama was not suitable for mass production. But please don't get me wrong, I'm not saying "Older Way is better". Indeed, there were no such as thing Soft Brick 1000 years ago. Pyrometric cone was invented by a German engineer. We use smartphone to set alarm clock for 4am shift.

I would like to study all of those from old way to new way either I take them or not. In addition, I need to practice a lot to gain the necessary skillset in order to build good forms. One of my instructors said "learn basic knowledge and skillset. Then, you can do Picasso stuff". Bill Daley's suggestions in your story supports me to proceed in this slow learning path. Unless I have basics, I can not share knowledge with constructive arguments in the community. I guess that's why I started my BFA last year. Well, coming to Komori Gama is already like Tokoname situation for me. I felt I stretched a lot. Before arriving Huntingdon, I was nervous to be honest with you but you and your folks are so generous and welcoming. At least, I had 4 firings before your place. I'm so happy that I started my pottery in this country.

When I started learning, I didn't understand what people said to me but now I start understanding: "Carbon Trapping", "Reduction Cooling", or "Flashing". At the same time, I tried to have independent view on my results. In a sense, I try not to get into a pitfall like "I see more carbon trapped on rim. It's better"; however, I will not going to give up reproducing effort of what I like or what I aim for using techniques I learned. In this spring, I met a potter, Shinohara, in Shigaraki. He told me is the story about chocolate. This is a story from a physics professor who came to see his work. Shinohara asked him why he likes his works. He said, chocolate is designed to melt at body temperature. We, physicist, can explain the mechanism. But understanding the mechanism is different than our feeling of deliciousness. You know what, you just added another dimension to this story. Like you said, doing critique right after unloading is the worst timing because some of their aesthetic value needs time to be understood. I guess I have to grow up to taste bitter chocolate.

Currently, I'm interested in developing my preferences:

1. Shino with very fine wood ash melted nicely and some crystals developed this time

I know that we discussed glazing outside or not but let me put my preferred glazing from this time. I had good results with shino - I brought 3 variations, traditional white, penn state shino (salmon pink), and a shino with iron in it. A whiskey cup caught a lot of ash and melted nicely. One of a mug developed a lot of crystal with peachy flame mark around foot. This is something I never got at Trevor's anagama. I also got very good result on my jar. Ashes landed and very smooth transition from the shoulder to the body. I'll probably keep around 20% pots glazed with shino. While I keep trying slip decoration for another 50%. I might not going back to other glazes I brought this time such as Yellow Salt and so on. If I try something new, I would like to try Mino shino. Attached are photos from this firing.

2. Iron rich clay body

When I started, I brought brown clay pots without glazing. It's Sheffield's 4D and it contains redart, fire clay, and ball clay. Somewhat I liked the earthy red / dark brown / purple tone even the company website says suitable for cone 6 oxidation. But most likely, other potters didn't like the results. Some people told me not to use brown clay for wood firing. But I really liked the texture and I called Yaki-Imo, means baked Japanese yum potato skin. So I kept my series in my bathroom shelf. They often describe under fire but I like how Rob loaded this time because he suggested to bring brown clay works to the back of kiln. It's simple solution but mind opening to me. Next time, I would try to target specific location which temperature and amount of ashes I prefer.

When I had lunch in your house, I found a bowl like mine, and you explained to me that's from your grad student. Then, you showed me your early works and Bizen vase. You commented possibly lower temperature firing like cone 9 as well. I didn't make many brown pots for this time but in the future I'll make more. I'm also excited to see what will happen about the clay you gave me. I start throwing tumblers and tea bowls , making more smooth surface. Attached photo is from my previous firing and your work.

  1. Torture zone

    The cup I asked people to drink green tea was from my last February firing. It was placed on top of the shelf, and it's cooked very well. I love those rough texture, and I try not to sand too much except for the rim part. This time, I got a bowl got ashes accumulated on slip, pretty much cooked gui-nomis, and yunomi.

4. Modified Saggar or Kimono

This is my idea connected to the 3 above. It's also connecting to my first discussion about why we use Anagama in 2023. I like those effects and phenomenon happening in wood firing; However, I would like to control effects each of my pots in the torture zone or even in front of the firebox. My aim is to have both an extremely rough surface and gentle texture together with some clear separation in certain contrast patterns.

My thought process is this. Ancient Chinese and Korean perfected beautiful celadon porcelain vase in saggar and they protected from wood ashes. Today, we would love to have wood ashes on our pots. Otherwise, we go straight gas kiln (again, don't get me wrong - I like gas firing and I didn't explore enough. University threw them away). So, in 2023, using complete closed saggar doesn't make sense to me unless aim for Hidasuki effects like bizen. Then, I thought, if wood firing pottery is the art of the flame, then can I spray paint of flame through a modified saggar as if it's stencil on it?

I don't know anybody does this similar approach, but I thought about this idea and its simply fun to plan for next few variations. It's very early stage idea for me. I called my saggars, Kimono, means clothes. Could be Tuxedo since I learn pottery in this country haha.

Using wadding / clam and base crown like your plates, I would like to try them all but a lot of ideas coming for next. Shrinkage rate, someone told me rub copper glaze inside, how about mini pocket and put charcoal or salt nearby? etc, etc.

5. Things I would like to try

I didn't work on porcelain that much. Since I saw many people use porcelain at Komori gama, I ordered B-Mix from Laguna. I'll see what I can make from this.

Another project is wild clay. I tried a few different types from long Island's north shore. I brought a few pieces in because they were secretly excavated by my instructor. This time, I put them in container but I'll try without it next time because they wouldn't melt.

  1. White - this one has some grog in it but is similar to white stone ware or some porcelain texture. must be a lot of feldspar in this layer. I didn't have enough to create one piece from this one.
  2. Yellow - some iron in it. I used for slip but didn't remain that much impact on the surface.
  3. Pink, Brown - must be a lot of iron. I made a small vase and totally black but not brittle. I like might adjust with something else to reduce iron. This could be a fun project to keep adjusting in every firing.
  4. Dark Gray - this layer grabbed my attention a lot. Although I saw big iron dots on white surface. The clay seems contains a lot of organic impurities but when I fired it as a bowl, the body was white / light brown. It also interacted with natural wood ash and atmosphere.

After writing those, ironically my mind went back to the chocolate story above. I will stop here about my preference. Perusing art in 2023 is such an odd thing. People around me suggest "Find your own voice", "Must be unique", or "Artist must be crazy". But it also restricts artists because we tend to avoid previous artist style.

Jumping back my thoughts about Mingei and contemporary art... that's why I kind a hesitate to explain Mingei as folk art. It's not bad way to introduce the concept but I think it is Yanagi's counter argument against modern individualism in art. Meanwhile, when I watched the documentary video of Kevin and I saw he stamped his maker's mark on the side, so I did same thing. It's my ego to show off my ugly maker's mark, haha. I guess we never be able to go back as anonymous potter, but I believe we can curve out our ethos in our pots. I hope my writing is not confusing but you are English teacher - I hope I get C+.

Sorry it took me a week to write this, and I must stop here. I'm still in the early stage and I am glad that you said this goes on for the rest of my life.

Thank you

Kiichi
